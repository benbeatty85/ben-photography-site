Japan

It is impossible to remain indifferent to Japanese culture. It is a different civilization where all you have learnt must be forgotten. It is a great intellectual challenge and a gorgeous sensual experience.

Alain Ducasse

Fujikawaguchiko

Our first stop in Japan would be a small town at the base of Mount Fuji. A touristy sort of place that acted as a basecamp for people wanting to conquer the monster that is Fuji. We stayed for two nights, the first day we spent exploring Aokigahara forest, a place of legends and darkness.

The Japanese believe this site is haunted by demons, a magnet for depressed people. While hiking this forest, we didn’t hear a sound the whole time, no birds or other animals, just erie silence.

Our second day we would tackle Fuji. We knew this was going to be difficult, but we had no idea how strenuous. Six hours to climb up and four hours down and many breaks in between. We did whats called a “bullet” climb, where you complete it in one shot without spending the night on the mountain.

There is an ancient Japanese proverb that states “He who climbs Mt. Fuji is a wise man; he who climbs twice is a fool.” What an unforgettable experience.

Tokyo

We had been wanting to visit Tokyo for a while, a city so large it’s almost incomprehensible. Tokyo offers a great balance of things to see and flavours to taste. We stayed in the neighbourhood of Asakusa. This was a great home base as it offered a very traditional feel and was a short walk from the famous Senso-ji temple.

I woke up at 4:30 am one morning so I could beat the tourist crowds at Senso-ji in order to capture the temple in a peaceful setting. During the day, the temple is overrun by people. I'm happy I was able to see it in a more tranquil state.

The sushi in Tokyo lives up to the hype, I visited Tsukiji twice, which is the largest fish market in the world. This is where you get affordable, mind blowing sushi in Tokyo. I also fell in love with Yakitory Alley, a confusing set of streets offering a great selection of grilled treats and tiny bars.

Our only night in Yakitory Alley, we sat next to a father and son in a small restaurant. They were from Tokyo and we could barely communicate but we spent the whole night buying each other sake and having a riot. For some reason, the communication seemed to improve as the collection of empty sake bottles grew in number.
