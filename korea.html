South Korea

Han, my favorite Korean word: It has many implied and specific meanings, but generally speaking, it's a mixture of endurance, yearning, sorrow, regret, bitterness, spite, hatred, and a grim determination to bide your time until revenge can at last be enacted.

Busan

Busan is located at the very edge of South Korea, next stop is Japan. It is considered one of the most beautiful large cities in Korea and is known for it’s beaches and temples. Visiting twice while living in Korea, we really enjoyed our time here, it has a much more relaxed vibe than the rest of the country.

The English teaching jobs in Busan are coveted, apparently difficult to get due to the high demand. We admit, it would have been really cool to live in Busan.

I personally enjoyed visiting the Jagalchi Market, which is the largest fish market in Korea. The fish stalls and seafood restaurants seemed to go on forever, one of those experiences that reminded us, we were living in Asia.

We also visited the majestic Haedong Yonggungsa Buddhist temple. A famous temple in Busan, it was built overlooking the East Sea and is a photographers dream.

Seoul

The drive to Seoul from our hometown of Sejong City was around three hours, however, the train was only forty-five minutes. The trains in Korea are some of the fastest in the world. This led to many day trips and overnight stays in the bustling metropolis. Seoul is a great city for urban exploration, it contains many temples, back alley streets and hidden gems.

It is also a city that reminds you how insignificant we are as individuals due to it’s sheer size and insane amount of activity. You could spend days exploring just one of it’s major markets and still not do it justice.

A memorable experience for us was the DMZ tour, which was only a forty minute drive from the centre of Seoul. We couldn’t leave Korea without seeing North Korea with our own eyes. The tour took us to an underground North Korean tunnel that the South Koreans found, which was meant to infiltrate Seoul.

They also brought us to a look out spot where you could see North Korean land. The vibe was strange the entire trip, it felt like a care-free Disney tour instead of a tour of the most “dangerous” place on Earth.

Chungcheong

Our home region, we lived in Sejong City which at the time had a population of 200,000 people, small for Asian standards. Sejong City did not exist prior to 2009, as it was just farmland. The Koreans implemented this epic plan to move much of it’s government offices away from North Korean missile range in Seoul to Sejong City over the next decade. This meant that we essentially lived in a massive construction zone, where buildings would seemingly spring up overnight.

It had it’s benefits, our apartment was brand new and all of the amenities the city had to offer were also newly constructed. It felt like we were living in the future. Everything was equipped with Korean state of the art technology. We also had easy bike access to the Korean countryside, giving us ample opportunity to cycle next to rice fields and visit beautiful national parks.

We taught English to kindergarten students up to kids who were twelve years old. The days were long and work challenging. We enjoyed the interaction with the kids and we will never forget them for the rest of our lives, but you definitely earn your money teaching in Korea.

Looking back on the experience now, it’s something we are proud to have done and would suggest anyone else to do the same. Working and living in a drastically different culture changed us forever, giving us a greater global perspective and improved our overall patience.
